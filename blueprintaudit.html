Why eleven systems can't agree on what's happening in one venue.

DIAGNOSIS
Star Lanes is not one business running on one system. It's four businesses — bowling, arcade, food & bar, and events — running on eleven systems that don't talk to each other.

This page maps every system Star Lanes uses today, what each one is responsible for, and where two or more systems claim ownership of the same fact about your business.

§ 01

The four moments your day breaks

Each of these is a specific place where the systems force a human to step in — every week, sometimes every day. The dollars come at the end of the page. The friction is what you actually feel.

BLEED · 01

Event deposits booked as revenue

When a corporate group puts down $1,000 to hold a date, Square logs it as $1,000 in sales the day the card runs. Your books say it's a liability until the event happens.

Every deposit gets manually backed out at month-end and reclassified as deferred revenue. With ~30+ events per month, that's 30+ journal entries that should be one automated rule.

"Square actually processes that transaction but they count it as sales for us. We hold it as a liability. Square doesn't recognize that."
Time cost
~2 hrs
per week, per location
BLEED · 02

Beverage packages with no inventory comp

When a corporate group prepays for an unlimited premium bar package, the bartender still has to ring every drink so liquor inventory stays accurate. Those rings inflate revenue a second time.

Micros had a one-button "inventory comp" that handled this cleanly. Square doesn't. So every event with a beverage package needs a manual reversal — and the bigger the event, the bigger the cleanup.

"On Micros we literally had a button. There's no way to do it in Square. So we're running this and my gross sales — it doesn't know, and I don't know."
Time cost
~1.5 hrs
per week, per location
BLEED · 03

Cross-system sales reconciliation

Square reports gross. The books need net. Embed sells game cards as one transaction; the redemption inside the venue is another. Triple Seat tracks event packages on its own ledger. Each one is locally correct and globally incomplete.

Every week, somebody pulls four reports, lines up the numbers, and figures out which is the real one. That's not a spreadsheet job — that's interpretive work that has to happen before anything closes.

"We're constantly creating workarounds. We're adding numbers and subtracting numbers. It's almost archaic."
Time cost
~1 hr
per week, per location
BLEED · 04

Triple Seat → Square → R365 mapping fixes

R365 inherits whatever Square sends it. If Square's categories are messy — and event packages, venue rentals, and bundled F&B make them messy — the mess gets copied straight into the books of record.

Mapping corrections get made on the R365 side, not the source side. Same fix, every period. The migration from Compeat to R365 is a chance to fix this once — or to inherit the workaround forever.

"There's a tremendous amount of work to get to the point where we actually can have it set properly, and Square is limited on the events side."

The Operating Layer

The shape of the fix.

ANSWER
Block A showed why the systems collide. Block B put a number on it. This is what the fix actually looks like — and what it isn't.

The instinct, when systems don't talk, is to find the one super-tool that does everything. You've been running that search for over a year — Diverted River, vendor demos, every booth at IAAPA. Nobody has shown up with the answer because the answer isn't a new system. It's a thin layer above the systems you already trust.

This block describes that layer in plain language — what it is, what it pulls from, what it produces, and what it deliberately is not.

Read this with a pen. If anything looks wrong or missing, mark it. The architecture has to be right before the implementation options on the next page mean anything.

The principle

Don't replace your systems. Build a layer that makes them agree.

Square is good at being Square. Triple Seat is good at being Triple Seat. Conqueror runs your bowling. Embed runs your arcade. None of these need replacing — they need a shared brain above them that reads from each one, classifies what it reads, and produces a single version of the truth.

The layer is read-only first. Nothing changes about how your team rings a sale, books an event, or sells a game card on day one. The layer just listens, normalizes, and reports. Once it's right and you trust it, then it starts writing back — into R365, into your loyalty program, into the daily operator digest.

That order matters. Read first, write second. It's how the work earns trust before it touches anything that matters.

§ 03

Three things this has to do, no exceptions

Whatever gets built has to clear all three of these bars on day one. Anything that fails one of them is a different project — and not the one Star Lanes needs.

NON-NEGOTIABLE · 01

Nothing breaks on day one.

Read-only deployment. Your team rings transactions the same way. Square, Triple Seat, Embed, Conqueror operate exactly as they do today. The layer listens — nothing more — until you trust it.

NON-NEGOTIABLE · 02

Built for two locations, not one.

Every architectural choice gets made for the OSU build, not just Polaris. The day OSU opens, you connect a second instance and roll out a multi-location dashboard — you don't rebuild.

NON-NEGOTIABLE · 03

Owned by you, not the vendor.

Customer data, revenue ledger, integration code — all live in your environment. If you ever sell, the buyer gets the operating system. No vendor lock that costs you leverage at the table.

What this is not.

Worth being explicit. The layer above is narrow on purpose. These are the things it deliberately doesn't try to be — because trying would dilute the work and double the timeline.

× Not a replacement for Square, Triple Seat, or any of your POS systems. Those tools stay exactly where they are.
× Not an AI phone agent. You already heard the negative feedback from UWM rolling that out. I'm not going there.
× Not a website chatbot. Your agency is handling that on Go High Level. Out of scope here.
× Not an SEO or AI-search optimization play. You're already doing that work and doing it well.
× Not a marketing automation engine. Go High Level can keep doing campaigns; this layer just hands it cleaner customer data to work with.
× Not a one-vendor lock-in. If you change accounting platforms, HR platforms, or POS in the future, the layer adapts. Your sources can change without rebuilding the brain.
